Planetary Habitability, Global Convergence and Structural Transformation, 2026–2100

Lucas Chancel, Cornelia Mohren, Moritz Odersky, Thomas Piketty, Anmol Somanchi — 2026

Can the world achieve broad-based prosperity by 2100 while remaining within planetary boundaries? This project develops a welfare-theoretic framework to compare two structurally different long-run development pathways: a Sustainable Convergence scenario and a Productivist Convergence scenario, evaluated across eight world regions from 2025 to 2100.

Going beyond GDP, the framework values both consumption and leisure, and applies an explicit climate damage wedge that captures how rising temperatures reduce effective living standards even as raw output grows. The welfare comparison is expressed as a consumption equivalent: the permanent percentage change in consumption that would make an individual indifferent between the two scenarios.

Two Scenarios

Sustainable Convergence (SC)

Global convergence to high living standards through structural transformation toward less material-intensive production, shorter working hours, and rapid decarbonisation. Cumulative warming of approximately 0.4°C by 2100. Lower raw consumption but substantially more leisure and far lower climate damages.

Productivist Convergence (PC)

Convergence driven by continued output growth along current structural patterns, with longer working hours and slower decarbonisation. Cumulative warming of approximately 2.8°C by 2100. Higher raw output, but compounding climate damages roughly halve effective consumption by century’s end.

Main Finding

Sustainable Convergence is welfare-superior in every world region. At the global level, the welfare advantage is equivalent to an 8.4% permanent increase in consumption. The advantage is largest in regions with high climate exposure and large leisure differentials (MENA +39.9%, Russia/Central Asia +32.4%, Sub-Saharan Africa +27.2%), and smallest in East Asia (+1.1%) where the two scenario trajectories diverge least.

The result is robust across utility specifications, discount rates, and climate damage parameterisations. Even at a high social discount rate of 5%, the Sustainable Convergence pathway retains a meaningful welfare advantage.
